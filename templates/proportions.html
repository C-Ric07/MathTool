How to Solve Proportions With Unknown

It's easy to solve a proportions with an unknown value. Firstly, let's take a look at the structure of a proportion. We define as means the inner terms, or the terms near the ":", while we call the outer terms, or the top right and left slots, extremes.

For example:


4 : 8 = 12 : 24

In this case, 4 and 24 are the extremes, while 8 and 12 are the means.
To approach the actual calculation, verify your availability in terms of means or extremes. In particular, check if you have both the means, or the extremes. Next step, multiply the two means or the two extremes. Then, divide by the remaining value (the only one missing). Your unknown will be the result of these calculations.


For example:


4 : x = 12 : 24

In this case, we can notice that we have both the extremes, or outer values (4 and 24). Following the procedure, let's multiply 4 * 24. This is equal to 96. After this, simply divide by the only remaining value (12). 96 : 12 = 8. This is our unknown value, or x



Practical Explanation

When we deal with proportions, we are comparing two ratios. This means that we are constructing a similarity to balance, in order to mantain an equilbirium.

An intuitive example could be the following scenario: a teacher has to give 3 candies to each student. How many candies should they distribute, if there are 5 students involved? This could be simply solved with basic skills. However, to understand the concept of proportions, let's set the first ratio, or fraction, or division. As the teacher should give 3 candies to each student, the ratio will be 3 : 1 (3 candies to 1 student), or 1 : 3 (1 student to 3 candies), even if the first one is more logical. Now that we have built our first ratio, let's focus on the second one. How many candies should be distributed to 5 students. If three candies have to be given to each student, then 15 candies in total should be distributed to 5 students. This is intuitive, but it helps to understand the concept of proportionality. In this case, we could set the following proportion:


3 : 1 = 15 : 5

In other words, 3 to 1 is in relationship with 15 : 5. In this case, the first and third term are related to candies, while the second and the fourth are related to students. It could be useful to reason in this way. Notice how the starting rules apply. Proportions are just another way to write basic math, in a visual and intuitive way. For example, 15 * 1 = 15. This value divided by 5 gives 3, confirming the initial statements.

Another example could be based on the most common use of proportions in our daily lives: measuring ingredients while cooking. Let's take as an example the recipe of a chocolate cake for 5 people. 140 grams of flour are needed. But if the cake is for 7 people, how can I find out how many grams of flour I need? One approach could be to firstly find the grams for one person. This is intuitive as we simply need to divide 140 grams by 5, the number of people of the recipe. The result is 28 grams. 28 grams per person. Then, if I need the amount of flour for 7 people, I should only multiply 28 * 7, obtaining the total amount of flour needed (196 grams). Now that we have understood the basic concept behind this example, let's try to take another approach, based on the use of proportions. As suggested before, build up the first ratio. You could build up this ratio as grams : people or viceversa. In this case, let's consider that 140 grams are needed for 5 people, so 140 : 5. Now, we have to find the amount for 7 people. As we have to keep the same structure (bear it in mind), grams : people, let's plug in the new number of people (7).

In conclusion, we have:


140 : 5 = grams : 7

Then, simply find the amount of flour for 7 people by using the previous rules. As we have both the extremes, let's multiply them: 140 * 7 = 980. Then, divide by the remaining value (the one that we have not used yet in our calculations, 5). 980 : 5 = 196. Have you noticed something? The result is equal to the one calculated without proportions! This was another simple and practical example to get how to assemble a proportion, when and how to use it.

For our last example, let's consider geometry, and a slightly more advanced scenario. We have two similar triangles. This means that the proportion between the corresponding sides is constant. In other words, the figures are the same, in terms of shape, but the sizes are changing. For this example, let's take two similar triangles ABC. In triangle 1, segment AB is 15 cm. In triangle 2, the corresponding segment AB is 20 cm. The other available data is the lenght of segment BC in triangle 1, which is 18 cm. We want to find che lenght of the corresponding side BC, in triangle 2.




Now It's time to set up our first ratio. Decide the order and the structure. Let's build it up as base : sloping edge, or AB : BC. So, for triangle 1, 15 : 18. For triangle 2, we know the base (AB), but the sloping edge is our unknown, so 20 : x. We are comparing these two ratios, because the division between the two sides must be equal in both triangles. So let's make up an equation between the two ratios, or in other words, a proportion.


15 : 18 = 20 : x

Then, let's solve for x, our unknown, following the initial rules. 18 * 20 = 360. Then, divide by the other number. 360 : 15 = 24 cm. This is the length of BC, in triangle 2, or x. It is a reasonable result, right?

We are saying that the proportionality between our 4 values is constant (if one value changes at the numerator or denominator of one side of the equation, then a change will be needed to mantain this proportionality).
